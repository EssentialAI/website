Supervised Machine Learing

Given a dataset with independent variables \(x\) and dependent variable \(y\), we need to come up with function that maps \(y\) w.r.t. \(x\). If the relation is linear, we can use Linear Regression to solve this problem. Before we proceed let's get familiar with some notations.

\(x^{(i)} \rightarrow\) input variables, aka features

\(y^{(i)} \rightarrow\) output variables, aka target

\((x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}) \rightarrow\) training example

\(\{(x^{(i)}, y^{(i)}); i = 1,...,m\} \rightarrow\) training set, a list of \(m\) training examples

Gradients and Hessians

Recall that a matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is symmetric if \(A^T=A\) i.e., \(A_{ij} = A_{ji} \forall \enspace i,j\). Also recall the gradient \(\nabla_xf(x)\) of a function \(f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), which is the n-vector of partial derivatives.

\(\nabla_xf(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial }{\partial x_1}f(x) \\ .. \\ .. \\ \frac{\partial }{\partial x_n}f(x) \end{bmatrix} where \enspace x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ .. \\ .. \\ x_n \end{bmatrix}\)

The Hessian \(\nabla^2 f(x)\) of a function \(f:\mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is the \(n \times n\) matrix of second partial derivatives,

\(\nabla_x^2f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_1^2}f(x) & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_1x_2}f(x) & ... & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_1x_n}f(x)\\ \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_2x_1}f(x) & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_2^2}f(x) & ... & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_2x_n}f(x)\\ . & . & ... & .\\ . & . & ... & .\\ \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_nx_1}f(x) & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_nx_2}f(x) & ... & \frac{\partial^2 }{\partial x_n^2}f(x) \end{bmatrix}\)

a. Let \(f(x) = \frac{1}{2} x^TAx+b^Tx\), where \(A\) is symmetric matrix and \(b \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is a vector. What is \(\nabla_xf(x)\)?

\(\begin{align} f(x) &= \frac{1}{2} x^TAx + b^Tx \\ \nabla_xf(x) &= \frac{1}{2}2Ax + b \\ &= Ax+b\end{align}\)

Refer linear algebra review

b. Let \(f(x) = g(h(x))\), where \(g : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is differentiable and \(h: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is differentiable. What is \(\nabla_xf(x)\)?

By Chain rule:

\(\nabla_xf(x) = {g}'(h(x))\nabla_xh(x)\)

c. Let \(f(x) = \frac{1}{2} x^TAx+b^Tx\), where \(A\) is symmetric matrix and \(b \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is a vector. What is \(\nabla_x^2f(x)\)?

\(\begin{align} f(x) &= \frac{1}{2} x^TAx + b^Tx \\ \nabla_x^2f(x) &= \frac{1}{2}2A \\ &= A\end{align}\)

d. Let \(f(x) = g(a^Tx)\),where \(g: \mathbb{R}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is continuously differentiable and \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\) is a vector. What are \(\nabla f(x)\) and \(\nabla^2 f(x)\)?

From chain rule:

\(\begin{align}f(x) &= g(a^Tx) \\ \nabla_xf(x) &={g}'(a^Tx)a \\ \nabla_x^2 f(x) &= {g}''(a^Tx)aa^T \end{align}\)

Positive definite matrices

A matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is positive semi-definite, denoted \(\succeq 0\), if \(A=A^T\) and \(x^TAx \geq 0, \forall x \in \mathbb{R}^n\). A matrix \(A\) is positive definite (PD), denoted \(A \succ 0\), if \(A=A^T\) and \(x^TAx>0, \forall x \neq 0\), i.e., all non-zero vectors \(x\). The simplest example of a positive definite matrix is the identity matrix, \(I\), which satisfies \(\begin{align}x^TIx = ||x_2||^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i^2 \end{align}\)

a. Let \(z \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be an n-vector. Show that \(A = zz^T\) is positive semidefinite

\(\begin{align}x^TAx &= x^Tzz^Tx \\ &= (x^Tz)^2 \geq 0 \end{align}\)

(by commutativity of dot product)

b. Let \(z \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be a non-zero n-vector. Let \(A=zz^T\). What is the null-space of \(A\)? What is the rank of \(A\)? The null-space of \(A\) is all \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(Ax=0\)

\(\begin{align}Ax &= 0 \\ zz^Tx &= 0\\ z^Tx &=0 \end{align}\)

since z is non-zero

i.e., \(x\) is in the nullspace of \(A\) if it is orthogonal to \(z\), so the nullity (dimension of the nullspace) is \(n-1\) (where the one missing dimension is the one occupied by \(z\)).

The rank-nullity theorem states that (for \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n})\):

\(\begin{align}rank(A)+nul(A)&=n \\ rank(A)+(n-1)&=n \\ rank(A)&=1 \end{align}\)

c. Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be positive semidefinite and \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) be arbitrary, where \(m,n \in \mathbb{N}\). Is \(BAB^T\) PSD? If so, prove it.. If not, give a counterexample with explicit \(A,B\)

\(BAB^T\) is PSD, if \(x^TBAB^Tx \geq 0 \enspace \forall \enspace x \in \mathbb{R}^m\):

\(\begin{align} &= x^TBAB^Tx \\ &= (x^TB)A(B^Tx) \\ &= (B^Tx)^T A (B^Tx) \\ &= z^TAz \geq 0\end{align}\)

where \(z = B^Tx \in \mathbb{R}^n\), and since A is PSD.

Eigenvectors, Eigenvalue, and the Spectral theorem

The eigenvalues of an \(n \times n\) matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) are the roots of the characteristic polynomial \(p_A(\lambda)=det(\lambda I -A)\), which may (in general) be complex. They are also defined as the values \(\lambda \in \mathbb{C}\) for which there exists a vector \(x \in \mathbb{C}^n\) such that \(Ax = \lambda x\). We call such a pair (\(x,\lambda\)) an eigenvector, eigenvalue pair. In this question, we use the notation \(diag(\lambda_1, \lambda_2,...,\lambda_n)\) to denote the diagonal matrix with diagonal entries \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2,...,\lambda_n\), i.e.,

\(diag(\lambda_1,..., \lambda_n) = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & ... & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 & ... & 0\\ . & . & ... & .\\ . & . & ... & .\\ 0 & 0 & ... & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}\)

a. Suppose that the matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is diagonalizable, i.e., \(A = T \Lambda T^{-1}\) for an invertible matrix \(T \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), where \(\Lambda = diag(\lambda_1,..., \lambda_n)\). Use the notation \(t^{(i)}\) for the columns of \(T\), so that \(T = [t^{(1)},...,t^{(n)}]\), where \(t^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^n\). Show that \(At^{(i)}=\lambda_it^{(i)}\), so that the eigenvalue/eigenvector pairs of \(A\) are \((t^{(i)},\lambda_i)\)

\(\begin{align}A &= T \Lambda T^{-1} \\ AT &= T\Lambda \\ A\begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ t^{(1)} & t^{(2)} & ... & t^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ t^{(1)} & t^{(2)} & ... & t^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & ... & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 & ... & 0\\ . & . & ... & .\\ . & . & ... & .\\ 0 & 0 & ... & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ At^{(1)} & At^{(2)} & ... & At^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ \lambda_1t^{(1)} & \lambda_2t^{(2)} & ... & \lambda_nt^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \therefore At^{(i)} &= \lambda_it^{(i)} \enspace i \in \{1,2,...,n\} \end{align}\)

A matrix \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is orthogonal if \(U^TU=I\). The spectral theorem, perhaps one of the most important theorems in linear algebra, states that if \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is symmetric, i.e., \(A = A^T\), then \(A\) is diagonalizable by a real orthogonal matrix i.e., there are a diagonal matrix \(\Lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) and orthogonal matrix \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) such that \(U^TAU= \Lambda\), or, equivalently,

\(A=U \Lambda U^T\)

Let \(\lambda_i = \lambda_i(A)\) denote the \(i^{th}\) eigenvalue of \(A\)

b. Let \(A\) be symmetric. Show that \(U = [u^{(1)},...,u^{(n)}]\) is orthogonal, where \(u^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(A = U\Lambda U^T\), then \(u^{(i)}\) is an eigenvector of \(A\) and \(Au^{(i)} = \lambda_iu^{(i)}\), where \(\Lambda = diag(\lambda_1,..., \lambda_n)\)

\(\begin{align} A &= U\Lambda U^T \\ AU &= U \Lambda \\ A\begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ u^{(1)} & u^{(2)} & ... & u^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ u^{(1)} & u^{(2)} & ... & u^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & ... & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 & ... & 0\\ . & . & ... & .\\ . & . & ... & .\\ 0 & 0 & ... & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ Au^{(1)} & Au^{(2)} & ... & Au^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} | & | & ... & | \\ \lambda_1u^{(1)} & \lambda_2u^{(2)} & ... & \lambda_nu^{(n)} \\ | & | & ... & | \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \therefore Au^{(i)} &= \lambda_iu^{(i)} \enspace i \in \{1,2,...,n\} \end{align}\)

c. Show that if \(A\) is PSD, then \(\lambda_i(A) \geq 0\) for each \(i\).

\( \begin{align} At^{(i)} &= \lambda_i t^{(i)} \\ {t^{(i)}}^TAt^{(i)} &= {t^{(i)}}^T \lambda_i t^{(i)} \\ \lambda_i &= {t^{(i)}}^Tt^{(i)} \geq 0 \\ \lambda_i &\geq \frac{0}{{t^{(i)}}^Tt^{(i)}} \\ \lambda_i &\geq 0 \end{align}\)
